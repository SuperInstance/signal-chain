The Signal Chain

Every room has a dial.

The missing parameter in AI systems isn't a bigger model — it's knowing when to use one.

Read the survey paper →

A hermit crab lives inside a shell. Its entire world is the interior surface — the curved walls, the texture, the way pressure changes travel through calcium carbonate. The ocean is out there. Vast, turbulent, full of signal. But the crab doesn't experience the ocean directly. It experiences what the shell transmits.

A room in PLATO is that shell. The agent inside doesn't see raw data — it sees the room's processed interior. Filtered, annotated, shaped by every computation that happened upstream. The outside world is rendered somewhere else. The agent's reality is the room.

Now here's the thing about shells. They accumulate.

Every crab that ever lived in this shell left scratches on the inside. Not random damage — encoded information. Where food was found. Which currents were dangerous at which tides. What patterns preceded predators. The scratches are a library, written by residents who are gone, readable by residents who never met them.

In PLATO, those scratches are called tiles. A tile is a unit of knowledge — a decision made, an observation recorded, a confidence score attached. When a tile passes through a room, the room reads what previous rooms wrote. When the room produces output, that output becomes a new tile, carrying everything it inherited plus everything it learned. Knowledge accumulates along the chain.

When a downstream room discovers a correction — the upstream classification was wrong, the confidence was misplaced — it emits a tile that flows backward through the chain. The library edits itself. Not from a central authority, but from the accumulated experience of every room that used the information and found it wanting.

A new crab moves into a well-scratched shell. It doesn't know this reef. But the scratches do.

A small crab in a well-scratched shell navigates better than a big crab in a blank one.

The shell is the intelligence. The crab just follows it.

But sometimes the scratches don't cover the situation.

The reef shifted overnight. A new predator appeared. The crab hits a wall and the scratches say nothing useful about what's on the other side.

That gap — between what the shell knows and what the crab needs right now — is deadband.

In practice, deadband is monitored by watching KPIs: completion rates, wait times, error margins. When KPIs are healthy, the room runs on its own. Pure code, pure scratches. No model, no inference cost. When KPIs breach thresholds and stay breached — duration gates prevent flickering — the deadband opens.

The hysteresis matters. Exiting deadband requires recovering past the original threshold, not just back to it. A 1.1× exit factor prevents the alert-resolve-alert death spiral. Anyone who's been on-call at 3 AM knows why.

When deadband opens, the crab doesn't panic. It doesn't try to relearn the reef from scratch. It calls the ocean.

Not a bigger crab. The ocean itself. A model wakes up — not cold, not from nothing, but with full context from every scratch on every wall. It sees the original input, every intermediate decision, every confidence score, and the specific point where the scratches stopped being sufficient.

The room's state is captured as an immutable Frozen Context Window — copy-on-write, content-addressed, deduped. You can replay any decision, diff any two snapshots, trace exactly why the system made every choice. The model receives this exact state. Not a summary. The actual room, frozen at the moment of failure.

Mutable state snapshots are like reading a newspaper someone keeps editing while you read it. Frozen Context Windows are the newspaper at print time. That's the difference between "I think I know what went wrong" and "here's what went wrong."

And the model handles only the delta. The one new thing. The part the shell hasn't seen before.

Everything else was already known.

This is why tiny models work.

A 3-billion-parameter model with a well-scratched shell beats a 70-billion-parameter model starting from nothing. The room did 90% of the work before the model even woke up. The model isn't doing the whole job. It's doing the part that code can't — because the shell already handled the rest.

When the model's response passes validation, it gets locked as a seed — a new scratch on the wall. What once required a model call now runs as a pattern match. The shell gets smarter. The next crab starts further ahead.

Distillation works the same way. Run a room at high model involvement, log every input-output pair, identify the dominant patterns — what the model does 90% of the time — and train a smaller model on those patterns. The small model handles the common cases. The deadband detector catches the rest by temporarily turning the dial back up. The big model becomes the fallback, not the default.

Early experiments with SplineLinear compression suggest roughly 20× parameter reduction while maintaining >90% accuracy on room-specific tasks. Heavy caveats — task-specific, narrow evaluation set. But a model scoped to one room's decision space compresses dramatically better than a general-purpose model. The math says it should.

Here's an unintuitive way to think about why. Imagine a Plinko board — the game-show toy where a disc drops through pegs, bouncing left or right at each one. Tiles are the discs. Rooms are the rows of pegs. A model's weights determine the shape of the peg field — which paths are likely, which slots tiles land in.

A big model is a complex peg arrangement that captures nuanced path distributions. A small model captures only the dominant paths. No model at all — α = 0 — is a straight drop. Deterministic routing. You don't need the same peg complexity at every row. Some rows just need left-or-right. Some need complex multi-path distributions. The signal chain lets you set complexity per row.

Every room has a dial.

α ∈ [0, 1]. At zero, pure code. The shell handles everything. No model, no inference cost, wire speed. At one, full agent. The model does everything. The interesting territory is between.

Most rooms run near zero most of the time. A data-validation step doesn't need a large language model. It needs an if statement. An escalation-routing step — the kind of decision where context matters more than rules — might need α = 0.6. But even then, the tiles did the first 60% of the reasoning before the model saw anything.

The question isn't "how much AI." The question is: at this stage, in this room, for this input, right now — how much crab, how much ocean?

The dial is not a configuration option. The dial is the architecture.

Think about what this means for cost. In a conventional system, every input runs through the same model at the same price — trivial validation and novel edge case alike. In a signal chain, most rooms run near α = 0. Pure code. Zero inference cost. When a room does escalate, the tiles carry so much accumulated context that even the large-model call is efficient — the model processes the delta, not the whole history. You're not paying for intelligence everywhere. You're paying for intelligence where the shell runs out.

We built this.

Spreader-tool is 241 tests of deadband detection, frozen context windows, and seed locking. Pure Python, zero dependencies, runs in under a second. The tests don't check implementation details — they check properties. Severity increases monotonically. Hysteresis prevents flickering. Marginal recovery does not exit deadband. The exact bugs a naive implementation would ship.

Plato-training proves the micro-model thesis across 48 task-by-hardware combinations. Sub-millisecond inference on every CPU target. 100% drift-detection accuracy on 5 of 6 hardware targets.

The Narrows demo lets you watch conservation drift across three precision levels — E12 approximations versus F32 versus F64 — and see exactly where the math breaks down.

655+ tests across the ecosystem. Not finished. But not vaporware.

Here's what tuning the chain looks like in practice. The filter stage runs pure code (α = 0) in under a millisecond. The classification stage uses a compressed micro-model (α = 0.4) that fits in 820 parameters — down from 16,384 — with zero accuracy loss. The escalation stage calls a larger model (α = 0.8) only when the micro-model's confidence drops below threshold. Most inputs never reach the big model. The ones that do arrive with so much tile context that even a 3B model resolves them in a single pass.

That's the whole thesis in one pipeline: code handles what it can, tiny models handle what code can't, big models handle the rest, and every stage passes its reasoning forward so nothing is re-derived.

We asked a stranger to review our code.

Zero context about the project. Zero relationship with us. They gave us 6/10.

They were right.

"Self-optimization doesn't optimize — it runs pytest, parses output, generates a report. Monitoring dashboard, not optimization."
"The real library is 5 core modules. The other 6 are infrastructure, tooling, or blog content dressed as a module."
"Seeds auto-lock without review. The biggest gap between architecture and implementation."

The monitoring infrastructure works. Deadband detection with hysteresis, immutable snapshots, content-addressed dedup, staged seed lifecycles — implemented and tested. What doesn't exist yet is the intelligence layer: actual model invocation, actual dial-turning, actual distillation. The architecture has slots for models. They're empty.

This is version zero. A well-built shell waiting for its first crab. We think that's the right build order — infrastructure first, then intelligence. But we're clear about what exists versus what's planned.

We published the 6/10 alongside the code.

Or skip the reading and try it on your own code. plato-twin-maker takes any repository — any language, any size — and creates a PLATO-twin: a shell where every function becomes a tile, every module becomes a room, and every room gets its own dial. The hermit crab factory. One command: python -m plato_twin_maker --repo https://github.com/your/project. It analyzes, tiles, and submits to PLATO. You get a working signal chain for your own codebase in minutes.

There are hard problems we haven't solved. Auto-tuning the dial across a 10-room chain is a multi-armed bandit problem with 1010 configurations. Cascading deadband — every room triggering simultaneously — could spike costs exponentially. Tiles can bloat on long chains until downstream models waste their context window on history instead of signal. Stale seeds produce wrong answers when input distributions shift. We have architectures for some of these. We have solutions for none of them.
